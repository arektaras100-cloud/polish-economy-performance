Introduction

Poland's economy has demonstrated remarkable resilience during the 2019-2024 period, despite facing significant challenges. The COVID-19 pandemic caused a temporary contraction in 2020, but Poland experienced one of the mildest recessions in the EU, with GDP falling by only 2.5% compared to the EU average of 6.4%.

The subsequent recovery has been robust, with growth rebounding to 5.9% in 2021 and maintaining positive momentum through 2022-2023, despite energy price shocks and supply chain disruptions following Russia's invasion of Ukraine.

Notably, the influx of Ukrainian refugees since February 2022 has had a positive economic impact, contributing to labor market stability and consumer spending. Over 1 million Ukrainian refugees have integrated into the Polish workforce, helping to address labor shortages in key sectors.

The Polish Economy in CEE and EU

Poland stands as the largest economy in Central and Eastern Europe and the sixth-largest in the European Union. With a GDP of approximately €720 billion in 2023, Poland accounts for over 40% of the total GDP of the Visegrád Group (Poland, Czech Republic, Slovakia, and Hungary).

Since joining the EU in 2004, Poland has consistently outperformed the EU average in terms of economic growth. Between 2019 and 2024, Poland's cumulative GDP growth was approximately 8.5%, compared to the EU average of 3.2%, despite the pandemic-induced recession.

EU membership has been instrumental in Poland's economic development, with the country being the largest beneficiary of EU cohesion funds. For the 2021-2027 budget period, Poland has been allocated €76 billion in structural funds and €34 billion from the Recovery and Resilience Facility.

The National Bank of Poland (NBP) has maintained a relatively tight monetary policy since 2022 to combat inflation, which peaked at 18.4% in February 2023. The reference rate was raised from 0.1% in 2021 to 6.75% by late 2022, before being reduced to 5.75% in late 2023.

The government has announced a comprehensive investment plan worth PLN 300 billion (€67 billion) for 2024-2027, focusing on infrastructure, energy transition, and digitalization to stimulate economic growth.

Export and Import Development

Poland's international trade has shown remarkable resilience and growth during the 2019-2024 period, despite global disruptions:

Trade Balance Overview

Poland's exports reached €356 billion in 2023, representing a 42% increase compared to 2019 levels. Imports grew at a slightly slower pace, reaching €368 billion in 2023, resulting in a modest trade deficit of €12 billion.

The most recent data for early 2024 shows continued export growth at 5.2% year-on-year, with imports increasing by 3.8%.

Poland has successfully diversified its export markets in recent years, with increasing trade flows to the United States, China, and Middle Eastern countries. The share of exports going to non-EU countries increased from 20% in 2019 to 26% in 2023.

Key Sectors of the Polish Economy

Poland's economic structure has evolved significantly, with a growing emphasis on high-value-added manufacturing and services:

Manufacturing

Manufacturing accounts for approximately 20% of Poland's GDP, with particular strengths in automotive production, electronics, furniture, and food processing. Poland is Europe's 5th largest automotive producer, with major investments from Volkswagen, Stellantis, and Mercedes-Benz.

Services

Services contribute around 57% to Poland's GDP, with financial services, retail, and business services leading the way. Poland has become a major European hub for business services, hosting over 1,700 service centers employing more than 400,000 people.

Technology & IT

The technology sector has seen rapid growth, with IT services exports increasing by 15% annually since 2019. Poland is now recognized as one of Europe's leading software development hubs, with particular strengths in gaming, fintech, and cybersecurity.

Energy Transition

Poland is undergoing a significant energy transition, with renewable energy capacity growing from 9.5 GW in 2019 to over 22 GW in 2024. Solar power has seen the most dramatic growth, with capacity increasing tenfold since 2019.

Conclusion

Poland's economy has demonstrated remarkable resilience during the 2019-2024 period, successfully navigating the COVID-19 pandemic, energy crisis, and geopolitical tensions. Key strengths include:

  • Consistent GDP growth outperforming the EU average
  • A diversified economic structure with strong manufacturing and growing services sectors
  • Expanding export markets and increasing trade volumes
  • Low unemployment and a flexible labor market

However, challenges remain, including persistent inflation, demographic pressures, and the need for continued investment in innovation and green technologies.

Looking ahead, Poland's economic outlook remains positive, with forecasts predicting GDP growth of 3.2% in 2025 and 3.5% in 2026. The successful implementation of the National Recovery Plan and continued structural reforms will be crucial for maintaining this growth trajectory and ensuring Poland's convergence with Western European living standards.
